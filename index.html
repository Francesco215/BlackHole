Why a universe can't be flat and infinite

The entropy of a black hole is S_b=\frac{k_bc^3}{4G\hbar}A=\alpha V^{2/3} where \alpha=\frac{k_bc^3}{4G\hbar}\sqrt[3]{\pi}\bigg(\frac34\bigg) and $A$ and $V$ are the area and the volume of the black hole, Therefore the entropy per unit volume of a black hole is s_b=\frac {S_b}V=\frac\alpha{\sqrt[3]{V}} And it decreases as the volume of the black hole gets larger and larger.

The main property of black holes that we are going to keep in mind is that a black hole of volume $V$ has the maximum entropy any spherical volume $V$ can have. Suppose we have a universe enclosed in a volume of radius $R$ and that on large scales we can say that it has constant entropy density $s_u=\frac{dS_u}{dV}$. Therefore the maximum volume $V_{max}$ of this universe is. V_{max}=\bigg(\frac \alpha{s_u}\bigg)^3 Therefore the univere can’t be infinite if it is homogeneus on large scales and flat

Why it expands

Suppose the volume of the Universe for some reason is exactly $V_{max}$ it would last a blink of an eye before collapsing into a black hole. That is because the total entropy has to increase. If the universe has some kind of law that keeps it’s volume always equal to $V_{max}$ we would have an expanding universe. This could explain why our universe in expanding, and why the expantion of the universe isn’t constant throught the age of the universe.

However this would mean that the universe has some kind of edge. This seems off, but we can start from this to make a better theory for universes that are not flat.

Another way of interpreting the rate of expation of the universe it that the expation on the universe is such that the entropy of the cosmologiacal event horizon must be equal of less to the entropy of the suff contained inside

What happens in hyperbolic space

Let's consider a simple hyperbolic space in two dimentions, the circumference $C$ and the area $A$ are C(r,\tau)=\frac{2\pi}{\tau} \sinh (r\tau) \quad \quad A(r,\tau)=\frac{4\pi}{\tau^2} \sinh^2 ( r\tau/2) Where $\tau$ represents how much the space is hyperbolic, for $\tau=0$ the space is flat. Controllare che le formule siano corrette, il modo in cui le ho ricavate io è cambiando unità di misura

We can imagine that is this 2D universe the maximum amount of entropy is proportional to the circumference $S_{max}(r,\tau)=s_b C(r,\tau)$.

Now we can imagine that our 2D universe has a constant entropy density per unit area $S_u(r,\tau)=s_uA(r,\tau)$. Just like before we must impose that S_u(r,\tau)\le S_{max}(r,\tau) Now some quick calculations... s_u\frac{4\pi}{\tau^2}\sinh^2(r\tau/2)\le s_b \frac{2\pi}{\tau} \sinh(r\tau) \frac 2\tau \frac{\sinh^2(r\tau/2)}{\sinh (r\tau)}\le \frac{s_b}{s_u} And we have that \frac 1\tau\tanh(r\tau/2)\le \frac{s_b}{s_u} This must be true for all $r$, since $\max\big[\tanh(r)\big]=\tanh(\infty)=1$ we have that for hyperbolic space s_u\le s_b\tau So that we have a more generalized upper limit to the density of entropy per unit volume of the universe. Notice how for $\tau=0$ we get the result that the universe must have $s_u=0$, which is the result we obtained previously.
